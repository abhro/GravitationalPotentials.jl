Potential due to an uniform density cylinder

Case 1: Test point on-axis

We use the cylindrical coordinate system, where $\mathbf{r} = (s, φ, z)$ (as in Griffiths' Introduction to Electrodynamics). By on-axis, we mean that our test point has the constraint $s = 0$, or, $\mathbf{r} = (0, φ, z)$. Note that technically $φ$ is not well-defined for $s = 0$, but since we have azimuthal symmetry in this system, this hiccup will be irrelevant.

In general, we can get the gravitational potential as

\[\Phi(\mathbf{r}) = -G \int_\text{cylinder} \frac{ρ(\mathbf{r}')}{\left|\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r}'\right|} \, d^3 \mathbf{r}'\]

where each $\mathbf{r}' = (s', φ', z')$ is a source point inside the cylinder, and the density function is

\[ρ(\mathbf{r}) = \begin{cases} ρ_0, & \text{if } \mathbf{r} \in \text{cylinder} \\ 0, & \text{if } \mathbf{r} \notin \text{cylinder} \end{cases}\]

Also note, in the cylindrical coordinate system, we have the two relations:

\[\begin{align*} \left|\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r}'\right| &= \sqrt{s'^2 + (z - z')^2}, & d^3 \mathbf{r}' &= s' \, ds' \, dφ' \, dz' \end{align*}\]

If we characterize the cylinder as having radius $R$ and height $2H$, the integral becomes

\[\begin{align*} \Phi(\mathbf{r}) &= - G \int_0^{2π} dφ' \int_0^R s' \, ds' \int_{-H}^{H} dz' \frac{ρ_0}{\sqrt{s'^2 + (z - z')^2}} \\[1ex] &= - 2π G ρ_0 \int_{-H}^H dz' \int_0^R \frac{s' \, ds'}{\sqrt{s'^2 + (z - z')^2}} \\[1ex] &= - 2π G ρ_0 \int_{-H}^H dz' {\left[\sqrt{s'^2 + (z - z')^2}\right]}_{s'=0}^{s'=R} \end{align*}\]

where we made use of the tabulated integral

\[\int \frac{x \, dx}{\sqrt{x^2 + k}} = \sqrt{x^2 + k} + C.\]

Plugging in the bounds for $s'$, we have

\[\Phi(\mathbf{r}) = -2π G ρ_0 \int_{-H}^H dz' \left(\sqrt{R^2 + (z - z')^2} - \left|z - z'\right|\right)\]

We can now use two more tabulated integrals:

\[\begin{align*} \int \sqrt{k + (p - x)^2} dx &= \frac{x-p}{2} \sqrt{k + (p - x)^2} - \frac{k^2}{2} \operatorname{artanh} \left(\frac{p-x}{\sqrt{k + (p - x)^2}}\right) + C \\ \int \left|p-x\right| dx &= \frac{x (2p-x) \operatorname{sgn}(p-x)}{2} + C \end{align*}\]

and we get

\[\begin{align*} \Phi(\mathbf{r}) = - 2π G ρ_0 \Biggl[ &\frac{z' - z}{2} \sqrt{R^2 + (z-z')^2} \\ &- \frac{R^2}{2} \operatorname{artanh} \left(\frac{z-z'}{\sqrt{R^2 + (z-z')^2}}\right) - \frac{z' (2z - z') \operatorname{sgn}(p-x)}{2} \Biggr]_{z'=-H}^{z'=H} \end{align*}\]

\[\begin{align*} \Phi(\mathbf{r}) = -G π ρ_0 &\Biggl[ \hphantom{-} \hphantom{-} (H - z) \sqrt{R^2 + (z-H)^2} - R^2 \operatorname{artanh} \frac{z-H}{\sqrt{R^2 + (z-H)^2}} - H (2z-H) \operatorname{sgn}(z-H) \\ & - (-H-z) \sqrt{R^2 + (z+H)^2} + R^2 \operatorname{artanh} \frac{z+H}{\sqrt{R^2 + (z+H)^2}} - H (2z+H) \operatorname{sgn}(z+H) \Biggr] \end{align*}\]

\[\begin{align*} \Phi(\mathbf{r}) = - G π ρ_0 & \Biggl[ (z+H) \sqrt{R^2 + (z+H)^2} - (z-H) \sqrt{R^2 + (z-H)^2} \\ & + R^2 \left(\operatorname{artanh}\frac{z+H}{\sqrt{R^2 + (z+H)^2}} - \operatorname{artanh}\frac{z-H}{\sqrt{R^2+(z-H)^2}}\right) \\ & - H \left((2z+H) \operatorname{sgn}(z+H) + (2z-H) \operatorname{sgn}(z-H)\right) \Biggr] \end{align*}\]
